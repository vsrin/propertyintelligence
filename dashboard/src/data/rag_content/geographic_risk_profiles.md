# Geographic Risk Profiles

## Florida

### Primary Perils
1. **Hurricane** - Entire state exposed, especially coastal areas
2. **Flood** - Low elevation, high water table, heavy rainfall
3. **Storm Surge** - Gulf and Atlantic coasts highly vulnerable
4. **Sinkholes** - Central Florida "Sinkhole Alley" (Pasco, Hernando, Hillsborough counties)
5. **Tornado** - Highest tornado density per square mile in US

### Regional Variations

**South Florida (Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach)**
- Highest hurricane risk zone in continental US
- Miami-Dade has strictest building code in nation (post-Andrew)
- High terrorism risk in Miami due to international profile
- Storm surge zones extend miles inland
- Flood zone A/AE common even inland
- Building code: Florida Building Code, High Velocity Hurricane Zone

**Central Florida (Orlando, Tampa Bay)**
- Sinkhole risk highest in state
- Tampa Bay has not had direct major hurricane since 1921
- Lightning capital of US - significant equipment damage risk
- Theme park concentration creates unique liability exposures
- Flood risk from afternoon thunderstorms year-round

**Northeast Florida (Jacksonville)**
- Moderate hurricane risk
- St. Johns River flooding concerns
- Lower insurance costs than South Florida
- Growing population increasing exposure concentration

**Panhandle (Pensacola, Panama City)**
- High hurricane risk, often overlooked
- Hurricane Michael (2018) caused catastrophic damage
- Less stringent building codes historically
- Military installation concentration

### Key Underwriting Considerations
- Roof age critical (15+ years = higher rates)
- Opening protection (shutters/impact glass) significantly affects pricing
- Distance to coast primary rating factor
- Sinkhole coverage excluded or limited in most policies
- Citizens Insurance as market of last resort
- Assignment of Benefits (AOB) litigation concerns

### Typical Risk Control Focus
- Hurricane shutters or impact-resistant openings
- Roof condition and attachment verification
- Flood zone determination and elevation certificate
- Sinkhole history for Central Florida locations
- Secondary water barrier requirement

## California

### Primary Perils
1. **Earthquake** - San Andreas and numerous other fault systems
2. **Wildfire** - Expanding wildland-urban interface
3. **Flood** - Atmospheric rivers, mudslides post-fire
4. **Tsunami** - Coastal areas, especially Northern California
5. **Drought** - Secondary impacts on structures and vegetation

### Regional Variations

**Southern California (Los Angeles, San Diego)**
- Multiple active fault systems (San Andreas, Newport-Inglewood)
- Santa Ana winds drive extreme wildfire conditions
- Urban-wildland interface expanding into fire zones
- Mudslide risk after fires strip vegetation
- Air quality issues affect sensitive operations

**San Francisco Bay Area**
- Hayward Fault runs through densely populated area
- Liquefaction risk in filled areas (Marina District, Oakland)
- High property values concentrate exposure
- Tech industry creates business interruption concerns
- Older URM building stock in urban cores

**Central Valley (Sacramento, Fresno)**
- Lower earthquake risk than coast
- Agricultural operations dominate
- Levee failure flood risk along Delta
- Extreme heat events increasing
- Air quality from Valley wildfires

**Wine Country (Napa, Sonoma)**
- High wildfire risk (2017, 2020 major fires)
- Unique high-value contents exposure
- Business interruption from fire season
- Tourism-dependent economy vulnerable
- Limited evacuation routes

### Key Underwriting Considerations
- CEA (California Earthquake Authority) capacity
- Earthquake retrofitting status for pre-1980 buildings
- Wildfire defensible space compliance
- Fire Protection Class (rural areas often Class 9/10)
- Brush clearance requirements
- FAIR Plan availability for fire-declined risks

### Typical Risk Control Focus
- Seismic vulnerability assessment
- Soft-story retrofit requirements
- Defensible space creation and maintenance
- Fire-resistant building materials
- Non-structural anchoring (equipment, shelving)
- Emergency water supply for firefighting

## Texas

### Primary Perils
1. **Hurricane** - Gulf Coast from Brownsville to Beaumont
2. **Tornado/Hail** - North Texas "Hail Alley"
3. **Flood** - Flash flooding statewide, coastal surge
4. **Freeze** - 2021 Winter Storm Uri demonstrated vulnerability
5. **Wildfire** - West Texas and Panhandle

### Regional Variations

**Gulf Coast (Houston, Corpus Christi, Beaumont)**
- Hurricane Harvey (2017) demonstrated flood vulnerability
- Petrochemical industry concentration creates contingent BI
- Storm surge extends far inland due to flat terrain
- Subsidence in Houston area worsening flood exposure
- No zoning in Houston complicates exposure assessment

**Dallas-Fort Worth Metroplex**
- Highest hail loss frequency in nation
- Spring tornado season (March-May peak)
- Rapid growth creating new exposures
- Older building stock in urban cores
- Large-loss hail events becoming more frequent

**Austin/Central Texas**
- Flash flood "Flash Flood Alley" designation
- Hill Country topography accelerates runoff
- Wildfire risk west of I-35
- Rapid tech industry growth
- Freeze exposure (Uri impact)

**West Texas/Panhandle**
- High wind exposure year-round
- Wildfire risk from dry conditions
- Sparse fire department coverage
- Oil/gas industry concentration
- Freeze/winter storm exposure

### Key Underwriting Considerations
- Named storm deductibles on Gulf Coast
- Hail deductibles in North Texas (often 1-2% of coverage)
- Freeze damage exclusions or limitations
- Distance to coast for hurricane rating
- Construction type critical for hail losses
- TWIA for coastal declined risks

### Typical Risk Control Focus
- Impact-resistant roofing in hail zones
- Hurricane mitigation on Gulf Coast
- Flash flood awareness and barriers
- Freeze protection for pipes and equipment
- Backup power for extended outages

## New York

### Primary Perils
1. **Terrorism** - New York City iconic target status
2. **Flood** - Coastal storm surge, inland riverine
3. **Hurricane/Nor'easter** - Long Island and coastal areas
4. **Freeze** - Pipe freeze in older buildings
5. **Windstorm** - Coastal and high-rise exposure

### Regional Variations

**New York City (Manhattan, Boroughs)**
- Highest terrorism risk in US
- Storm surge demonstrated by Sandy (2012)
- Aging infrastructure (100+ year old buildings)
- High-rise concentration creates unique risks
- Congestion complicates emergency response
- Subway flooding impacts business interruption
- Construction defect litigation prevalent

**Long Island**
- Direct hurricane/nor'easter exposure
- Coastal erosion concerns
- Sandy devastation in South Shore communities
- High property values in Hamptons
- Limited evacuation routes

**Hudson Valley**
- Riverine flood from Hudson River
- Historic building stock
- Ice dam issues in winter
- Moderate seismic risk

**Upstate (Buffalo, Syracuse, Rochester)**
- Lake effect snow creates roof collapse risk
- Freeze/pipe burst primary concern
- Legacy industrial contamination
- Declining population in some areas

### Key Underwriting Considerations
- Terrorism Risk Insurance Act (TRIA) considerations
- Flood insurance requirements expanding post-Sandy
- Building age and systems condition
- Local Law 11 facade inspection compliance (NYC)
- Sprinkler requirements in high-rises
- Co-op/condo board requirements

### Typical Risk Control Focus
- Security and access control (terrorism)
- Flood barriers and emergency procedures
- Facade maintenance (Local Law 11)
- Fire protection system maintenance
- Pipe freeze prevention
- Emergency generator capacity

## Illinois (Chicago Focus)

### Primary Perils
1. **Tornado/Hail** - Severe convective storms
2. **Freeze** - Extreme cold, pipe burst
3. **Flood** - Chicago River, flash flooding
4. **Wind** - "Windy City" high-rise exposure
5. **Fire** - Aging building stock, historic fires

### Regional Characteristics

**Chicago Metro**
- High-rise concentration along lakefront
- Deep tunnel flood control system (TARP)
- Historic building stock (pre-1900)
- Lake Michigan wind exposure
- Cold weather infrastructure stress
- Rail and aviation hub (business interruption concerns)

**Suburban Areas**
- Tornado exposure in western suburbs
- Flash flood from rapid development
- Newer construction generally
- Data center concentration (contingent BI)

### Key Underwriting Considerations
- Building age (Chicago Fire 1871 + rebuild era)
- Heating system condition critical
- Flat roof construction (ponding, collapse)
- High-rise wind loads
- Basement flood exposure

### Typical Risk Control Focus
- Freeze prevention and monitoring
- Roof load management (snow removal)
- Tornado safe rooms/shelter areas
- Flood barriers for lower levels
- Wind bracing verification for high-rises

## Massachusetts (Boston Focus)

### Primary Perils
1. **Nor'easter** - High winds, flooding, snow
2. **Hurricane** - Less frequent but possible (1938, 1954)
3. **Flood** - Coastal and riverine
4. **Freeze** - Extended cold periods
5. **Earthquake** - Low but non-zero seismic risk

### Regional Characteristics

**Boston Metro**
- Historic building stock (1700s-1800s)
- Fill areas (Back Bay) have unique risks
- Universities create unique exposures
- Healthcare/biotech concentration
- Limited parking complicates emergency access
- Big Dig infrastructure concerns

**Cape Cod/Islands**
- Direct hurricane exposure
- Coastal erosion accelerating
- High-value seasonal properties
- Limited emergency services
- Evacuation constraints

### Key Underwriting Considerations
- Historic building requirements
- Coastal flood zone expansion
- Nor'easter damage potential
- Building systems age
- University/institutional portfolios

### Typical Risk Control Focus
- Nor'easter preparation
- Flood barriers for coastal properties
- Historic preservation requirements
- Freeze prevention systems
- Backup power for critical facilities

## Washington State (Seattle Focus)

### Primary Perils
1. **Earthquake** - Cascadia Subduction Zone, Seattle Fault
2. **Volcano** - Mt. Rainier lahar threat
3. **Flood** - Heavy rainfall, river flooding
4. **Landslide** - Steep terrain, saturated soils
5. **Windstorm** - Pacific storms

### Regional Characteristics

**Seattle/Puget Sound**
- Cascadia megathrust potential (M9.0+)
- Unreinforced masonry common in Pioneer Square
- Liquefaction risk in fill areas
- Tech industry concentration
- Lahar zones from Mt. Rainier
- High rainfall but infrastructure adapted

**Eastern Washington**
- Wildfire risk
- Flash flood from thunderstorms
- Agricultural exposures
- Less seismic risk than coast

### Key Underwriting Considerations
- Seismic retrofit status critical
- Landslide hazard areas mapped
- Flood zone for river proximity
- Lahar evacuation zone awareness
- Tech industry business interruption

### Typical Risk Control Focus
- Seismic vulnerability assessment
- Non-structural anchoring
- Landslide risk evaluation
- Flood preparedness
- Emergency communication plans

## Common Multi-Region Considerations

### Climate Change Impacts
- Increasing hurricane intensity
- Wildfire season lengthening
- Flood zone expansions
- Freeze events in unexpected areas
- Heat-related equipment failures

### Portfolio Aggregation
- Geographic concentration limits
- Correlated loss potential
- Reinsurance treaty triggers
- Probable Maximum Loss (PML) calculations

### Regulatory Environment
- State-specific building codes
- Insurance availability programs (FAIR, Citizens, TWIA)
- Disclosure requirements
- Rate regulation variations
