# Risk Control Recommendations by Occupancy Type

## Office Buildings

### Risk Profile
Office buildings generally present moderate fire risk due to limited fuel loads and regular occupancy during business hours. Primary concerns include electrical systems, tenant improvements, and life safety for high-rise configurations.

### Common Hazards
- Electrical fires from equipment overload
- Cooking fires in break rooms/cafeterias
- Tenant improvement work creating temporary hazards
- After-hours security and arson concerns
- Slip/fall exposures in lobbies and common areas
- HVAC system failures affecting operations

### Standard Recommendations

**Fire Protection**
- Maintain automatic sprinkler system per NFPA 25
- Inspect portable fire extinguishers monthly
- Test fire alarm system quarterly
- Prohibit space heaters or require UL-listed units only
- Ensure electrical panels accessible (36" clearance)
- Install surge protection for sensitive equipment

**Life Safety**
- Conduct annual fire drills
- Maintain illuminated exit signage
- Keep egress paths clear of storage
- Test emergency lighting quarterly
- Post evacuation maps on each floor
- Train floor wardens/fire safety directors

**Security**
- Implement access control during and after hours
- Install CCTV in common areas and parking
- Screen visitors and contractors
- Secure server rooms and sensitive areas
- Conduct background checks for cleaning staff

**Premises Liability**
- Maintain flooring in good condition
- Install adequate lighting in parking areas
- Address snow/ice promptly
- Inspect exterior walkways regularly
- Document maintenance activities

### High-Rise Specific (75+ feet)
- Maintain stairwell pressurization systems
- Test fire pump annually with flow test
- Verify elevator recall operation monthly
- Maintain fire command center equipment
- Coordinate with fire department annually

## Retail / Mercantile

### Risk Profile
Retail occupancies have higher fire loads from merchandise and packaging, significant public traffic creating premises liability exposure, and theft/shrinkage concerns. Seasonal variations affect both inventory values and foot traffic.

### Common Hazards
- Stock room fires from carton accumulation
- Electrical overload from seasonal lighting
- Slip/fall from customer traffic
- Cart/equipment injuries in parking lots
- Theft, shoplifting, and organized retail crime
- Product liability from merchandise sold

### Standard Recommendations

**Fire Protection**
- Install sprinklers in stock rooms and sales floor
- Limit carton storage height (18" below sprinklers)
- Remove empty cartons daily
- Control seasonal lighting installations
- Prohibit extension cord permanent use
- Store flammable goods properly (aerosols, etc.)

**Stock Room Management**
- Maintain clear aisles (minimum 44")
- Stack merchandise stable and secure
- Keep materials 18" below sprinkler deflectors
- Store hazardous materials in approved cabinets
- Limit palletized storage height to rack capacity

**Premises Safety**
- Inspect flooring daily for hazards
- Clean spills immediately with documented protocol
- Maintain adequate lighting throughout store
- Install mats at entrances during wet weather
- Clear snow/ice from walkways and parking
- Repair cart corrals and parking lot surfaces

**Security**
- Install EAS (electronic article surveillance)
- Position checkout to observe exits
- Implement cash handling procedures
- Secure high-value merchandise
- Train staff on robbery response

### Shopping Mall/Large Format Specific
- Coordinate fire safety with anchor tenants
- Maintain food court ventilation and suppression
- Control tenant hot work permits
- Manage common area special events
- Ensure adequate emergency exits

## Manufacturing / Industrial

### Risk Profile
Manufacturing presents the highest fire severity potential due to processes, equipment, and materials. Risk varies dramatically by specific manufacturing type. Key concerns include process hazards, equipment protection, and workers' compensation exposures.

### Common Hazards
- Process fires (welding, heating, chemicals)
- Equipment breakdown and failure
- Dust explosions (combustible dust)
- Chemical releases and spills
- Material handling injuries
- Electrical equipment failures

### Standard Recommendations

**Fire Protection**
- Design sprinkler system for specific hazards
- Install dust collection with explosion venting
- Protect flammable liquid storage (FM approved)
- Maintain hot work permit program
- Install equipment-specific suppression (spray booths, etc.)
- Separate high-hazard processes with fire walls

**Process Safety**
- Conduct hazard analysis for all processes
- Implement lockout/tagout procedures
- Maintain equipment per manufacturer specs
- Train operators on safe procedures
- Document all process changes

**Housekeeping**
- Clean combustible dust per NFPA 652
- Manage oil and debris accumulation
- Control raw material storage
- Properly dispose of waste materials
- Maintain clear egress paths

**Equipment Protection**
- Implement predictive maintenance program
- Monitor critical equipment temperatures/vibration
- Protect against power quality issues
- Maintain spare parts for critical equipment
- Document equipment inspection/maintenance

### Specific Manufacturing Considerations

**Woodworking**
- Dust collection with spark detection
- Separation of finishing from woodworking
- Proper storage of stains/finishes
- Equipment guarding

**Metal Working**
- Quench tank maintenance
- Coolant/cutting oil management
- Welding area fire protection
- Hydraulic system inspection

**Plastics/Chemicals**
- Process hazard analysis
- Emergency response planning
- Proper chemical storage
- Ventilation systems

## Warehouse / Distribution

### Risk Profile
Warehouses present high fire severity due to concentrated commodity storage. Rack storage configurations and ceiling heights significantly impact fire development. Forklift operations create both property and liability exposures.

### Common Hazards
- High-rack storage fires (rapid vertical spread)
- Forklift collisions (racks, personnel, product)
- Dock door security and theft
- Product contamination
- Slip/fall in picking areas
- Cold storage specific hazards

### Standard Recommendations

**Fire Protection**
- Design sprinkler system for commodity class and storage height
- Install in-rack sprinklers for high-rack storage
- Maintain 18" clearance below sprinklers
- Control idle pallet storage
- Limit high-hazard commodity concentration
- Ensure dock areas protected

**Rack Storage**
- Install rack protection (column guards)
- Conduct annual rack inspections
- Repair damaged racks immediately
- Post load capacity signage
- Train forklift operators on rack awareness

**Forklift Safety**
- Certify all operators per OSHA
- Inspect forklifts daily
- Maintain pedestrian separation
- Enforce speed limits
- Charge batteries in designated areas with ventilation

**Security**
- Control dock access
- Implement seal verification procedures
- Install cameras at dock doors
- Screen trailer contents
- Inventory management controls

### Cold Storage Specific
- Maintain ammonia detection systems
- Inspect refrigeration equipment regularly
- Address ice accumulation promptly
- Ensure emergency procedures for ammonia release
- Verify adequate insulation integrity

## Hospitality (Hotels/Motels)

### Risk Profile
Hotels combine residential sleeping, assembly (ballrooms), and commercial (restaurants) occupancies. Life safety is paramount due to transient occupants unfamiliar with layouts. Brand standards often drive risk quality.

### Common Hazards
- Kitchen fires (commercial cooking)
- Guest room smoking (where permitted)
- Laundry fires (dryer lint accumulation)
- Swimming pool drowning/injuries
- Slip/fall in wet areas
- Guest security incidents
- Special events with temporary hazards

### Standard Recommendations

**Fire/Life Safety**
- Maintain automatic sprinklers throughout
- Test smoke detectors in guest rooms monthly
- Conduct fire drills quarterly for staff
- Post evacuation plans in guest rooms
- Maintain exit lighting and signage
- Train staff on fire extinguisher use

**Kitchen Operations**
- Maintain hood suppression systems (NFPA 17A)
- Clean hoods and ducts per NFPA 96
- Train staff on fire suppression activation
- Inspect grease filters weekly
- Control cooking oil storage

**Guest Safety**
- Maintain balcony railings and guards
- Ensure bathroom grab bars secure
- Address slip hazards in wet areas
- Provide adequate room lighting
- Maintain furniture in safe condition

**Pool/Recreational**
- Maintain lifesaving equipment
- Post depth markers and rules
- Ensure pool chemistry compliance
- Fence and secure pool areas
- Train staff in rescue procedures

**Security**
- Implement electronic key card systems
- Install cameras in common areas
- Provide in-room safes
- Control contractor access
- Train staff on incident response

### Special Events
- Require event certificates of insurance
- Control pyrotechnics and open flames
- Verify capacity limits
- Plan for crowd management
- Coordinate with local fire marshal

## Restaurant / Food Service

### Risk Profile
Restaurants have the highest fire frequency of commercial occupancies due to cooking operations. Kitchen hood and suppression system maintenance is critical. Premises liability from customer injuries is significant.

### Common Hazards
- Cooking fires (grease fires most common)
- Hood/duct fires from inadequate cleaning
- Burns and cuts to employees
- Slip/fall in kitchen and dining areas
- Food contamination and illness
- Liquor liability (if applicable)

### Standard Recommendations

**Kitchen Fire Safety**
- Maintain hood suppression per NFPA 17A/96
- Clean hood and ducts quarterly minimum (more frequent for high-volume)
- Install K-class fire extinguisher at fryers
- Train all staff on suppression activation
- Maintain fire-rated separation from dining

**Hood/Duct Maintenance**
- Clean per NFPA 96 schedule based on cooking type
- Document all cleanings with certification
- Inspect fans and ductwork during cleaning
- Ensure access panels operable
- Verify grease filter integrity

**Cooking Operations**
- Monitor fryer temperatures
- Filter/replace cooking oil regularly
- Never leave cooking unattended
- Keep combustibles away from cooking equipment
- Maintain equipment per manufacturer specs

**Slip/Fall Prevention**
- Install anti-slip mats in kitchen
- Clean spills immediately
- Require non-slip footwear for staff
- Maintain floor surfaces
- Control entrance area wet weather

**Food Safety**
- Maintain proper food storage temperatures
- Train staff on food handling
- Document temperature logs
- Control cross-contamination
- Ensure proper handwashing facilities

### Liquor Liability (If Applicable)
- Train staff in alcohol service (TIPS, ServSafe)
- Document refused service incidents
- Verify patron age carefully
- Monitor intoxication levels
- Arrange safe transportation options

## Healthcare Facilities

### Risk Profile
Healthcare facilities have 24/7 occupancy with patients unable to self-evacuate. Complex mechanical/electrical systems and stored medical gases create unique hazards. Regulatory compliance (Joint Commission, CMS) drives many requirements.

### Common Hazards
- Oxygen-enriched fires
- Medical gas system failures
- Electrical system failures affecting life safety
- Infection control breaches
- Patient falls and injuries
- Medical waste management

### Standard Recommendations

**Fire Safety**
- Maintain defend-in-place capability
- Test fire alarm and suppression systems rigorously
- Control oxygen storage and cylinder management
- Maintain fire/smoke barrier integrity
- Conduct fire drills per CMS requirements
- Train staff on RACE and PASS

**Medical Gas**
- Secure cylinder storage racks
- Maintain zone valve functionality
- Label all medical gas outlets
- Control smoking (zero tolerance)
- Inspect bulk storage regularly

**Electrical Systems**
- Test emergency power monthly
- Maintain transfer switches
- Verify critical branch coverage
- Document load testing
- Protect against power quality issues

**Life Safety**
- Maintain means of egress
- Test emergency lighting monthly
- Keep fire doors functional
- Control corridor storage
- Verify nurse call systems

**Regulatory Compliance**
- Prepare for Joint Commission surveys
- Document all safety activities
- Maintain Environment of Care committee
- Complete annual hazard vulnerability analysis
- File incident reports appropriately

## Educational Facilities

### Risk Profile
Schools have high occupant loads with children who require supervised evacuation. Science labs, kitchens, and maintenance shops present specific hazards. Summer vacancy creates security and maintenance concerns.

### Common Hazards
- Laboratory fires and chemical incidents
- Kitchen fires in cafeterias
- Arson and vandalism during vacancy
- Playground and athletic injuries
- Slip/fall in corridors and gyms
- Portable classroom exposures

### Standard Recommendations

**Fire Safety**
- Maintain automatic sprinkler systems
- Conduct fire drills monthly per code
- Control science laboratory chemicals
- Secure flammable storage
- Maintain portable classroom separation

**Laboratory Safety**
- Store chemicals properly (compatibility)
- Maintain fume hood operation
- Provide safety showers and eyewash
- Train teachers on chemical safety
- Limit chemical inventory

**Security**
- Implement visitor management
- Control building access
- Install cameras at entries
- Develop emergency response plans
- Coordinate with local law enforcement

**Premises Safety**
- Inspect playground equipment monthly
- Maintain athletic facilities
- Address floor hazards promptly
- Provide adequate corridor lighting
- Control snow/ice on walkways

**Summer/Vacancy Protection**
- Increase patrol frequency
- Maintain alarm systems
- Continue grounds maintenance
- Protect against vandalism
- Drain water systems if extended vacancy
